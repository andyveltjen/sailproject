## Synthesis literature review

Based on the analysed literature (n=87 publications, status March 2025), we formulate the following preliminary findings:

### 1. There is no universal approach
The literature clearly shows that a one-size-fits-all policy does not work. What is effective depends heavily on:
- The discipline (exact vs. human sciences)
- The level of the course (bachelor vs. master)
- The specific learning outcomes of a subject
- The digital literacy of the target group

### 2. Teachers need concrete tools
International studies (Baker et al., 2024; OECD, 2024) show that the biggest bottleneck is not the lack of AI knowledge, but the lack of **transferable pedagogical frameworks** that teachers can apply immediately.

### 3. Transparency is key
Institutions that make AI use transparent - both to teachers and students - report fewer academic integrity problems and higher learning efficiency.

### 4. Policy lags behind practice
In almost all countries surveyed, students are already using AI, while formal policy is still being developed. This creates a grey area that is detrimental to both students and teachers.

## Gaps in the literature

We note that the following areas are underrepresented in the literature:
- Longitudinal research on long-term effects on learning efficiency
- Studies in Dutch-language educational contexts
- Impact on vulnerable student groups
- Effectiveness of specific pedagogical interventions

*These gaps will help form the basis for our practical research in phase 2.
