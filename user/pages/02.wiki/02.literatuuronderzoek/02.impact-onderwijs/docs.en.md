## Introduction

The scientific literature on the impact of AI on learning processes is growing rapidly. We distinguish three domains: cognitive impact, motivational effects and social dimensions of learning.

## Cognitive impact

### Cognitive offloading
A recurring theme in the literature (Risko & Gilbert, 2016; Sweller, 2023) is **cognitive offloading**: the delegation of mental tasks to external resources. AI significantly amplifies this phenomenon.

**Risks:**
- Reduced development of basic skills (writing, arithmetic, reasoning)
- "Automation bias": blind acceptance of AI outputs
- Reduced memory usage and deep processing

**Opportunities:**
- More cognitive space for higher-order thinking
- Faster iteration and experimentation
- Scaffolding for weaker students

### Zone of Proximal Development
Vygotsky's concept of the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) has been reinterpreted in AI context in recent literature. Chan & Hu (2023) argue that AI can provide a unique form of scaffolding that adapts to the student's level - something that is difficult for the teacher to achieve in large groups.

## Motivational effects

A meta-analysis by Zawacki-Richter et al (2022) across 146 studies shows a **mixed picture**:

- Students report higher self-efficacy when AI provides direct feedback
- At the same time, intrinsic motivation decreases when AI takes over tasks completely
- Achievement-oriented students show stronger tendency to over-reliance on AI

## Social dimension

Learning is inherently social (Wenger, 1998). The literature warns of the risk of AI use undermining the social dimension of learning:

- Less peer interaction in AI-assisted assignments
- Risk of "AI isolation": student solves problems with AI rather than consulting fellow students
- Language acquisition research shows reduced output among students using AI for language production (Huang et al., 2024)

## Academic integrity

This is the most discussed topic in recent education literature. Thurlings & Zijlstra (2023) distinguish three positions:

| Position | Starting point | Implication for policy |.
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Ban approach** | AI = fraud | Detection tools, explicit bans |
| **Transition approach** | AI = new reality | Adjust assignments, allow AI if justified |
| **Integrative approach** | AI = opportunity | explicitly incorporate AI use as learning activity |

The international consensus is shifting towards the **integrative approach**, where AI use is used transparently and purposefully as part of the learning process.
